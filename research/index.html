The overarching goal of my dissertation research is to create 10 m resolution forest inventories of New England and Atlantic Canada using LiDAR and satellite data. These inventories include estimates of biomass, percent conifer, tree density, basal area, tree height, percent spruce/fir, percent white pine, quadratic mean diameter, total volume, merchantable volume, and volume breakdowns by species groups. To achieve this my dissertation is broken down into four parts.

Chapter One: Novel Methods for Measuring LIDAR

The Objective of this chapter is to identify new methods for quantifying LiDAR point clouds which are less reliant on the industry standard height metrics. The metrics I have developed are primarily based on segmenting each tree’s crown within a plot, and then aggregating summary statistics to feed into a plot-level area based model. Some examples include mean crown volume, canopy rugosity, mean growing space, and light availability. It is my hope that the work done in this chapter will offer LiDAR modelers more ecological reasoning behind their models, as well as make models more transferable between different LiDAR datasets. Chapter 2: The Use of Three Dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks.

The goal of this chapter is to apply state-of-the-art neural networks to directly measure the LiDAR point cloud and make estimates of forest attributes. This is an important step forward because normally LiDAR models are based on metrics derived from the LiDAR (see chapter one), which are subject to change and lack complexity. A standard LiDAR inventory is often based on nothing more than the height of LiDAR points within an area. Convolutional neural networks are special in that they are able to scan the LiDAR automatically and ‘learn’ what to measure. Ultimately they are able to quantify complex shapes such as tree crowns. The results from this chapter indicate that this method produces more accurate results than traditional modelling approaches.

Ayrey, E., & Hayes, D. J. (2018). The Use of Three-Dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks to Interpret LiDAR for Forest Inventory. Remote Sensing, 10(4), 649.

Chapter Three: Synthesizing Disparate LIDAR and Satellite Data to Generate Regional Forest Inventories with Deep Learning.

The goal of this chapter is to pull together the results from chapter two and apply a convolutional neural network to develop forest inventories for all the public LiDAR in New England and New Brunswick. In addition to LiDAR data, spectral data from Sentinel, disturbance data from Landsat, and phenology data from MODIS have all been incorporated into the model. The model’s form resembles that of Google’s Inception V3, converted to three dimensions, with additional inputs for the satellite data and outputs for each of the forest metrics. This deep learning super-model has a much greater performance that standard LiDAR inventory models. Ultimately it is my goal to host these inventories on here for the public to make use of.

Chapter Four: Projecting LiDAR Through Time With the Use of Generative Adversarial Networks.

One problem with making a regional LiDAR inventory is that much of the region’s LiDAR is nearly ten years out of date. Recent advances in artificial intelligence have yielded excellent results for projecting human faces forward and backwards in time. My hope is to use this same technology to create the world’s first purely LiDAR-based growth and yield model. One could feed in a LiDAR point cloud from a particular date, and then project it any number of years into the past or future. I am also examining including Landsat disturbance history to allow the user to trigger a harvest. The resulting point cloud could then be interpreted using a LiDAR inventory model like those being developed in the first three chapters. Or it could aid in visualization of hypothetical forests landscapes.

As of July of 2018 my first two chapters are complete. Regional maps are currently being generated and will likely be complete by Fall of 2018. The GANs being used in chapter four are currently being played with. My anticipated graduation date is Summer of 2019. After which time I’ll likely be living in a box under a bridge, as this is an absurdly niche field and my degrees aren’t in computer science.
